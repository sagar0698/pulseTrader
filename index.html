How to buy — step by step
Buying a Stock
When you buy a stock, you own a small piece of the company. No expiry, hold as long as you want.
  1. Open Robinhood, Webull, or Fidelity.
  2. Search the ticker (e.g. AAPL for Apple).
  3. Choose Market Order (immediate) or Limit Order (set your max price).
  4. Enter dollar amount — fractional shares are fine.
  5. Review and tap Submit.
  6. Set a mental stop-loss (e.g. sell if −8%).
💡 Most brokerages allow fractional shares — you can own $25 of Apple without paying the full share price.
Buying a Call Option
A call gives you the right to buy shares at a set price before expiry. You profit if the stock rises above the strike.
  1. Go to the stock's Options chain.
  2. Select Call, pick the expiry from the pick card.
  3. Choose strike near current price (ATM or slightly OTM).
  4. Each contract = 100 shares. $1.50 premium = $150/contract.
  5. Start with 1 contract. Submit.
  6. Sell before expiry when profitable — don't let it expire worthless.
💡 Options can expire worthless. Never invest money you can't afford to lose. Start with 1 contract.
Buying a Put Option
A put gives you the right to sell shares at a set price. You profit if the stock drops below the strike.
  1. Go to the Options chain for the ticker.
  2. Select Put, pick the expiry from the card.
  3. Choose strike at or slightly below current price.
  4. $0.90 premium = $90/contract.
  5. Submit and monitor closely — puts decay fast near expiry.
  6. Sell the put when profitable rather than holding to expiry.
💡 Only buy puts when you have a specific reason to think a stock will fall. Don't guess.
Short-term vs Long-term
Short-term (days–weeks): Trading momentum. Enter near entry range, set target and stop-loss, exit when hit. Options are almost always short-term due to time decay.

Long-term (months–years): You believe in the company. Ignore short-term swings. Review quarterly, add on dips.
💡 If the pick says "sell by [date]" — take it seriously. Options lose value daily (theta decay), especially the last 2 weeks before expiry.
Starting on $20–$1,000
You don't need a lot to start. Here's how to be smart with a small budget:
  1. Start with fractional shares in stocks you know (Apple, Microsoft, S&P 500 ETF).
  2. For options, look for contracts under $1.00/share ($100/contract).
  3. Never put everything in one pick. Spread across 3–5 positions.
  4. Keep 20% as cash — always have money ready for better entries.
  5. Use paper trading first (Webull offers it free).
💡 Picks marked ⭐ Budget Pick in this app are under $300 — perfect for small accounts.
Risk management basics
The most important skill is not losing big.
  1. Never risk more than 2–5% of your account on a single trade.
  2. Always know your exit before you enter.
  3. Don't chase a stock that already ran up 5%+ in a day.
  4. Avoid trading options during earnings week unless experienced.
  5. If your stop-loss is hit, exit. Don't hold hoping for a reversal.
💡 It's better to miss a good trade than take a bad one. There's always tomorrow.

Step-by-step: how to buy your first option with $50
  • Open the options chain
    Search your ticker on Robinhood or Webull. Tap "Trade" then "Trade Options." You'll see a grid of strike prices and expiry dates.
  • Pick an expiry 3–4 weeks out
    Never buy options expiring in less than 7 days with a micro budget — time decay will destroy the value. Look for 21–30 days to expiry (DTE).
    Look for the column marked "Ask" — that's what you'll pay per share. Multiply by 100 to get your contract cost.
  • Find a premium under $0.50 ($50/contract)
    At your budget, look for ask prices between $0.20–$0.50. With $50, you can buy 1 contract. With $100, you can buy 1–2 contracts.
  • Place a limit order, not market
    Set your limit price at the midpoint between bid and ask. This gets you a better fill and avoids overpaying on the spread.
  • Set your sell rules BEFORE you buy
    Rule 1: Sell if the option doubles in value (100% gain). Rule 2: Sell if you lose 50% of the premium. Rule 3: Always sell at least 5 days before expiry.
    Write your rules down. Emotions will tell you to hold longer. Don't listen.

Sell rules for $100–$500 options
  • Take profits at 50–75% gain
    Don't be greedy. A 50% gain on a $200 position is $100 — that's real money. Sell half at 50% and let the other half ride to your target.
  • Cut losses at 30–40%
    If your option drops 35% in value, exit. Options can go to zero. Protecting capital is more important than hoping for a reversal.
    A 35% loss needs a 54% gain just to break even. Exit early and redeploy.
  • Time rules: exit 7–10 DTE
    With 7 days left to expiry, theta decay (time decay) accelerates dramatically. Sell your option with at least 7 days remaining, regardless of profit/loss.
  • Never hold through earnings unless intentional
    Earnings reports cause massive volatility. Unless you're specifically playing an earnings move (see Weekly Earnings Calls tab), close your position the day before earnings.

Advanced sell rules for $500–$2k
  • Scale out — don't exit all at once
    Sell 1/3 of your contracts at your first target (e.g. +40%), another 1/3 at +80%, and let the last 1/3 run to the full target. This locks in profits while staying in the trade.
  • Use a trailing stop
    Once your option doubles, set a mental trailing stop at 25% below the current value. If it pulls back 25%, exit. This prevents giving back all your gains.
    Example: option at $4.00. Your stop is $3.00. If it rises to $5.00, your stop moves up to $3.75.
  • Consider vertical spreads to reduce cost
    A bull call spread (buy a call, sell a higher-strike call) reduces your cost basis and limits max loss. Example: Buy AAPL $210 call, sell AAPL $215 call. Net cost ~$150 instead of $400.

Understanding the Greeks
  • Delta — how much the option moves per $1 stock move
    An ATM call has ~0.50 delta. If the stock rises $1, your option gains $0.50 (x100 = $50/contract). ITM options have higher delta (0.70–0.90) and behave more like owning shares.
  • Theta — time decay (your enemy)
    Theta is how much your option loses per day just from time passing. A $2.00 option with −$0.05 theta loses $5/day per contract. It accelerates in the last 2 weeks before expiry.
    Theta is why you never hold options too close to expiry. Sell with at least 7–10 days left.
  • Vega — sensitivity to volatility changes
    High vega means your option's value rises sharply if market volatility increases (like before earnings). This is why options get expensive right before earnings — IV (implied volatility) spikes.
  • Straddle — a way to profit from big moves in either direction
    Buy both a call AND a put at the same strike and expiry. Useful before earnings when you know a big move is coming but don't know which direction. Costs more but profits from volatility itself.
